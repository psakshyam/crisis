|  Explanation of Crisis  | Finalized Summary: The 2008 Financial Crisis happened when the U.S. housing market collapsed after many years of rising home prices and risky lending. Banks gave many people mortgages (loans used to buy homes) that they could not afford, and these loans were turned into complex financial products (investments created by banks that are bought and sold to make money). When home prices fell, many people could not repay their mortgages, causing large banks to fail and panic to spread worldwide. Because banks were closely connected, the problems quickly spread through the economy and led to a recession (a period when businesses slow down, many people lose jobs, and the economy shrinks). Governments stepped in with large bailouts to stabilize the financial system and reduce economic damage. Basic Facts (EBSCO): Timeline: Sept-Oct 2008  Place: U.S. & Europe \+ Asia  Status Quo: “starting in the summer of 2007, worsening conditions in the US housing and home-finance sectors led to an escalation of financial distress”  Impacts (Pew): “Americans place the blame for the current problems with financial institutions and markets squarely on people who took on too much debt and banks that made risky loans”  United Nations Projections “As long as domestic consumption has an influence on domestic output, this should also help reduce the volatility of overall output. Countries which are more financially open can more easily borrow from abroad, and therefore should be able to withstand a crisis – such as the recent one – better.” (p. 111\)  “The probability of entering a very deep recession might increase in proportion to the openness of the capital account. This is because capital controls are usually geared more towards short-term capital flows, and hence a more open capital account means a larger share of volatile short-term inflows in the overall capital inflows of a country.” (p. 112\)  "Emerging market economies and developing countries should think twice about opening up their capital accounts. Should they decide to open their capital accounts, countries should undertake active macroeconomic management to prevent the emergence of large current-account imbalances…” (p. 113\)   |

| 2020 COVID-19 Pandemic |  |

|  Explanation of Crisis  | Finalized Summary: In late 2020 COVID-19 Pandemic occurred sending the global market into a near permanent prolonged shock. Countries around the world were forced to balance humanitarian objectives with economic objectives, rarely satisfying officials responsible for the lifeblood of society: health, education and trade. It is widely accepted that this strain of COVID-19 originated from bats, and the very first cases sprung up in Wuhan, China in December of 2019, with each of them at the time attributed to sickness found at a local fish market. Within one month, China reported the first 40 official cases, and the first viral death. By March 11, 2020, the disease was declared a pandemic. Economic activity shut down globally, and freedom of mobility was tightly restrained by law, following slightly different measures around the world. In spite of intense measures, over 7.1 million individuals have succumbed to the virus since its introduction, and collective GDP fell by 3.4%. Countries, like that of Italy, spent almost half (46%) of their individual GDP as a fiscal response to be able to control the virus. In the United Kingdom, over 1.3 million people were laid off, and many others were forced to adapt to reduced working hours or remote work. In both Central Asia and Europe, businesses lost a quarter of their turnover. A two pronged attack was undertaken to curb the pandemic’s effects: the first vaccine trials began early in March, and the American Operation Warp Speed furthered development of competing vaccine candidates. As of November, the leading vaccines showed themselves to be 95-95.4% effective, as countries like the United States began to fund hundreds of millions of doses to be distributed amongst the public. By December 2020, the first Americans will receive vaccinations, and just ten days later, over one million Americans have been vaccinated. As vaccinations were slowly distributed across the world, first from the elderly to the youth, the vaccination effort and the economic recession were managed by the lowering of interest rates to near zero levels in Canada and The United States, increasing Quantitatve Easing and most importantly, Keynesian investments to both flood the market and bring monetary and disease management relief to the public through small business loans, forms of stimulus parallel to Universal Basic Income and vaccine development. The effects of COVID-19 last in both families, and the economy today, as many global economies that relied on borrowing power throughout the crisis have submerged themselves into national debt crises. Balancing the objectives of preserving human life, but also the human economic way of life presented the global economy with their toughest challenge since World War II. Basic Facts: Timeline: December 2019 \-May 2023  Place: Global  Status Quo: “ in January 2020, a natural virus found in Wuhan China, with similar symptoms to pneumonia, unresponsive to current treatment, registered its first case outside of China, in Thailand.” Impacts: “many place the blame for the development of the pandemic on the lack of preventative health measures and immunology work, which had been rolled back in favour of cost cutting measures which created a world more vulnerable to mass illness"  United Nations Projections from COVID-19 and Human Development: Assessing the Crisis, Envisioning the Recovery “But this pandemic has been unprecedented because of its evolution from a health shock to an economic and social crisis. Social distancing and the pause in nonessential business have slowed human activities. The International Labour Organization projects that in the second quarter of 2020, working hours will fall by the equivalent of 195 million full-time workers.. ” (p. 5\)  “In the absence of a vaccine or therapeutics, most of the measures to slow the spread of COVID-19 have been nonpharmaceutical interventions (figure 5). The strategy of reducing contagion aims not just to protect the most vulnerable populations, but also to avoid excessive pressure on health systems. Even countries with high numbers of hospital beds per 1,000 people can see health services become overwhelmed during the peak of a pandemic. 38 So, reducing virus transmission reduces the pressure on the health system and health workers, buying them time to increase and spread capacity.” (p. 8\)  "However, the COVID-19 crisis poses several special challenges to policymakers, both because of the pre-existing weaknesses of the global economy and because of the special characteristics of this crisis.107 Throwing resources at the economy might not suffice. As discussed, during a lockdown the priority is not to stimulate demand, as in a standard recession with a shortfall in aggregate demand. The key objective is to design policies that deal with the current compound crisis and promote inclusive human development in the coming years and for future generations. An equity lens is essential because existing inequalities mediate the impacts of the crisis on human development.” (p. 21\)   |
